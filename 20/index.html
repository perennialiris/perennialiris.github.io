When I speak to people who are very critical of Israel, they often characterize those who disagree with them, even mildly, as callous, evil, or genocidal, or as somebody who hates, is prejudiced against, or who discounts the lives of Arabs or Muslims. It's possible for somebody to arrive at a pro-Israel position because they think of the Palestinians as uncivilized, scum, or animals, who are incapable of peace, and whom they hope Israel just wipes out already. It's also possible for somebody who is strongly anti-Israel to be approaching from their own biases against the West or against Jewish people. They fixate on Israel because it's a Jewish country, while largely ignoring all the other wars, conflicts, and cruelty elsewhere in the world, including elsewhere just in the Arab world. They come to believe various conspiracy theories about Israel somehow controlling the West, controlling the American military and Congress, running our media. They claim to care about international law, but only when Israel is breaking it. They care about genocidal rhetoric, except when it's directed against Jews. They offer endless compassion and understanding to Palestinians who are born into the conflict, who have known no other life than Israeli occupation; but none for the innocent Jews born into Israel, who have known no life other than their small country being surrounded by militants who want them dead and fire missiles into their neighbourhoods hoping to kill anybody they can. I'm personally wary of either possibility. Palestinians will always be our fellow human beings, and how we treat people matters---including those among them who are actually in terrorist groups, which is not the majority. I do what I can to combat racist or dehumanizing speech in our community. I likewise try to be wary about how Jewish people are treated and spoken of, which in my case, because I tend to interact with more left-wing people than right-wing these days, has been the greater of these two struggles. In recent years the court of public opinion here in the West has shifted against Israel. This is partly due to changing circumstances as their military incursion into Gaza has unfolded, but also due to an alternative media sphere that's been increasingly influenced by organized propaganda campaigns. There are young people today getting all their information about the world from social media who don't seem aware of the extent of the anti-Israel bias they're surrounded by like fish in water. There are now whole networks of people, sometimes state-affiliated and sometimes not, who want to control the online narrative, pushing anti-Zionist views in every comment section across TikTok, Reddit, Quora, Twitter, YouTube, and so on---you name it, and there's group chats somewhere dedicated to brigading it. And there's a radicalization pipeline trying to pull people in a far-left, anti-Western direction in general, using this issue as its starting point. This has fuelled my desire to address the topic. An important thing to keep in mind these days is that modern disinformation campaigns don't operate as a top-down structure where an organized centre does all the work disseminating the desired beliefs. Instead they may help set the tone or direct the movement's focus, but to actually exert narrative control they rely to large extent on the emergence of a community of amateurs who enjoy feeling like they're researchers and activists who are part of something important, and who, in practice, end up functioning like a regime-controlled "troll farm" even without any explicit state affiliation (consider the online communities dedicated to spreading vaccine misinformation or which surrounded the "QAnon" movement, among other conspiracy-driven phenomena). That said, there are legitimate reasons for people in the West to be critical of Israel, even fixated on it compared with conflicts elsewhere in the world. We're all given to at least be *aware* of Israel when our history classes teach about World War II and the Holocaust, which could be a point of entry, as history elsewhere in the world does not receive the same level of emphasis. There is a perceived causal link between things happening here in the West and in the Middle East today. If there were a war happening in Myanmar and we believed that it was partly our fault, then people would be more likely to pay attention to it. A person who's generally unaware of the Israel--Palestine conflict might also hear about it each election cycle, when some of our politicians get on stage and clarify their support for it. I recall that in past elections here in Canada, the Conservative Party included as a point on its official platform that it was committed to support for Israel, mixed in with its statements about everything else. I understand why some party organizer thought to include the mention, but if I were somebody unfamiliar with the conflict and the political mess that surrounds it, that point could make me rather curious about just what it is we're so keen on supporting. Likewise, if every election members of our political class were expected to clarify whether or not they support the government of Andorra, then I would instantly want to know a lot more about what's happening down there than I currently do. This itself can make people curious: what could be so difficult about it? Why hasn't the world been able to get these two groups to stop fighting and killing each other after so long? Then, if you're the curious type, once you get into it you find so much depth and so many moving parts that it can keep you occupied for a long time. It leads to discussions about history, or about our feelings about imperialism and colonialism, to what sort of obligations we have to deal with the lingering effects of past injustice, to what a justifiable response can be in response to an enemy that puts their own people in danger, or what kind of response can be justified when fighting against a far more powerful enemy that's expelled you from your homes. **To talk about this topic in a productive manner** requires that we're able to listen to each other without assuming the worst intentions. Which I take as an invitation to no longer engage with that person, since if this is what they believe about me, then I don't know why they would want to talk with me anyway. ## Competing intuitions Imagine you know nothing about Israel--Palestine. You've never heard about it before, you have no bias, you haven't been influenced to think one way or another. If you started examining the conflict right now, or perhaps at a point in time 3 years ago, what would be your initial impression? It depends. The conflict invites different intuitions depending on who's asking and what direction your mind happens to go. Here are some possibilities: #### 1. Choose the side that does less bad stuff. This is how many people who end up being more pro-Israel operate. If there's a war between two sides and we have to choose which to support, a good way to start is to ask which side is less evil. In many ways the obvious answer is Israel when compared with either other countries in the region or with groups like Hamas or Hezbollah. They have a better system of government, better laws, better culture. They do better on women's rights, gay rights, religious tolerance, freedom of speech, democracy, adherence to international norms. Until 2014, a popular thing to note was that Israel was the only country in the region where homosexuality wasn't illegal; to this day they're still the most LGBT-friendly by far. They also commit many fewer atrocities and war crimes. If a normal person looks at Tel Aviv and then at cities in other countries in the region, they may quickly realize which side of the fence they prefer to be on. This moral calculation may change over time, depending on in what year it's made. #### 2. Choose the side that aligns with us. If one country is burning American flags and chanting death to America, while the other side is reaching out and saying they're willing to work with us, then maybe we should just pick the latter. Why make it more complicated? Israel is, apparently, a good ally to the United States. They do good intelligence work, they have a good tech industry. They provide us a good foothold in the Middle East. Prior to trying to examine the conflict itself, a person might just decide supporting Israel seems like the rational choice. #### 3. With great power comes great responsibility, or, a tendency to favour the underdog. If one side of a conflict is far more powerful and wealthy than the other, they're the ones who are really in control and they should ergo be held to a higher standard, and we should be examining the conflict primarily from their perspective. They're holding all the cards. We should also be careful about how much blame we put on people who are oppressed, because they have fewer options, be more desperate, and in cases where an unfair situation goes on for more than a generation, you'll have people being born into this conflict who know no other life, so they lack our modern perspective to guide their own actions. They have agency, but perhaps less culpability. #### 4. Anti-imperialism---or, bias for the native population. This is more common among the far-left than moderates, but some people look at the conflict and they see Israel as the more foreign country whose population are outsiders to the region, whereas the Palestinians are the people who were there first. This means even before any fighting occurs, if both groups claim a right to the same land, there is more legitimacy to the Palestinian claim, and ergo Palestinians are more justified in the ensuing conflict. Israel's very existence is moreover seen as part of or an extension of a broader Western colonial project, which further casts it in a negative light. There is a lingering feeling of injustice that comes from the feeling that Israel is itself built on stolen property. (The less-obvious problem with this view is that the view Israelis are foreigners to the region is factually inaccurate and generally considered to be anti-Israel propaganda, framing the Jewish people as being European or American transplants. From the beginning, there have always been many Israelis of Middle Eastern descent, and today the majority of Jewish people in Israel were born in Israel.) #### 5. Anti-Americanism---or, Anti-Western bias. This is similar to the previous point, and again a far-left tendency, especially for people who are coming from within the American sphere of influence. They criticize everything through the lens of not wanting America to go around imposing its influence onto the rest of the world, and will therefore tend to view conflicts through the lens of that which does and doesn't support American interests. Israel can be viewed as an extension of American interests, if not a puppet state of the US, a wedge the US is using to divide or destabilize the Middle East for its own purposes. Israel, they reason, should therefore be opposed. #### 6. Be sensitive to our own prejudice. We all have some bias or another, and some---racism, sexism, xenophobia---can affect how people judge a conflict between parties of different groups. Liberal people try to be aware of common prejudice and compensate for them in our evaluation of things. Not all prejudice is conscious and deliberate, but combatting prejudice generally has to be. If a fight happens at work between a man and woman in a way that seems to make others bias toward the man, we might stop and give extra consideration to the woman's point of view to ensure we're not overlooking her perspective out of sexism (even if a passive, 'accidental' sexism). Would we feel the same way about what happened if their roles were reversed but their actions weren't? If so, why? This kind of self-checking and reflection can be valuable. If you apply this to Israel--Palestine, there are some ways in which there's a danger of being biased toward Israel: Israeli is more similar to our society, they're more 'Western', many Americans are prejudiced against Muslims, we also tend to like wealthier people over poorer people, and Israelis are generally thought of or depicted as being more racially 'white' than the Arabs (this isn't really true---many Israelis are 'non-white'---but it's still part of how many people conceptualize the conflict). So automatically, we should place a higher level of scrutiny on the Israelis, to compensate for the ways in which we might just be being prejudiced against the other side. #### 7. Anti-Jewish bias. An unfortunate reality Jewish people face is being the perpetual victims of conspiracy theories. In the West, this has been present in people's views of Israel for decades---from speculation about the USS *Liberty* (a US Navy ship that was attacked by Israel in error during the 60s), to the notion that Israel somehow caused or had something to do with the Sept. 11 attacks on the World Trade Center, to the idea that Israel is to this day somehow calling the shots of American foreign policy through legitimate advocacy work by organizations like AIPAC. In the United States, a non-zero portion of people oppose doing anything to support Israel because they view Israel not as an ally that can help serve US interests, but instead as a bunch of sneaky Jews who are somehow working to manipulate or undermine US interests. If you look it up on Wikipedia, the estimate given is 750,000. That's the size of a reasonably large city today. It's roughly the same population as Seattle. The stronger case for there being an overlap in function or effect is not about Israel proper but the Israeli occupation of the West Bank. If your position is that Israel's actions in the West Bank are unjustified in some way, that could be a reasonable position, as I'll explain later. But why is Israel in the West Bank? It's not because there's a race-based caste system they're trying to impose. It's not because they're trying to attack people for being Muslims. Israel has sought good relations with other countries whose populations are more or less identical in genetics and religious belief. The Israeli presence in the West Bank is a military occupation that arose out of conflicts about borders and national defense. We could sure find things to compare it with in history, but it wouldn't be South Africa. Military occupation is an unspecific term for when a region is taken over by a military that's not its own. For example, during World War II, some parts of Germany were put under military occupation by Allied forces. Did they have to let the Germans in those towns vote in American elections while the military was there? If not, does that mean those Germans lived under apartheid? What about the idea that citizenship is based on race? It would be quite the trick if South Africa were to only grant citizenship to white people and then claim they're not enforcing racial barriers, but giving a normal amount of privilege to citizens. But there is no racial boundary in citizenship. Nor is there a religious one. There are Muslim, Palestinian Arabs who are Israeli citizens. They vote in Israeli elections. The stars are not aligning here. It is true that it's easier for people with Jewish ancestry to become citizens. There's also many other countries who extend this courtesy to their own diaspora throughout the world. Jewish people fled and spread out across other countries because they were a powerless, persecuted minority group. Now that there's finally a country where Jews have a stable population, is the idea that they're not allowed to offer their own people easier citizenship if they come home, otherwise that's a horrific system of racial domination? And yes, this isn't a unique policy. I don't think the fact it's easier for people with Irish ancestry to get Irish citizenship means Ireland is an apartheid state either. The less-obvious downside of having this conversation is the other, potential conversations that we could've been having instead. We should be talking about the military occupation of the West Bank for what it is. We think of a military occupation as something that can be justified temporarily in war, not as something that becomes a new state of affairs and goes on for another 50 years. Would it be acceptable if the US were still ruling over parts of Germany today? Perhaps if the territory were not merely occupied but annexed and taken over, made part of their country. But in that case, Israel *would* need to accept responsibility for the people of the West Bank. We should expect they be given Israeli citizenship and all rights that accompany it. But that's not what Israel is doing or interested in doing. It's not an annexation. They aren't declaring that the West Bank is part of Israel. And this sort of **strategic ambiguity** should be the focus of criticism. ### Indiscriminate bombing and 'dumb bombs' To kill *indiscriminately* means to not care who you're hurting, to make no distinction between your target and a casualty. For a military to kill indiscriminately is, in most contexts, a serious accusation. The nuclear bombs dropped on Japan killed Japanese people indiscriminately, meaning we didn't care *which* Japanese people we killed (though we did target Japan, as opposed to just any random humans on the planet, so I guess it's all about scope). To say that Israel is indiscriminately killing Palestinians would imply that Israeli forces are shooting or bombing any random Palestinian they see, without regard for who they are. I've never heard of any evidence that this is happening. Israel clearly cares about target selection. They don't just kill whoever they see. They try to target militants. If you feel that Israel ends up killing too many civilians (however you even determine that), then you should be outraged at Hamas for forcing Israel to fight in civilian areas, for the fact that Hamas deliberately fights in civilian clothing, and does nothing to protect their civilian population. The fact it's so difficult to engage in combat with Hamas without civilian casualties isn't merely Hamas's fault, it's the result that Hamas consciously wanted. Israel tries to minimize civilian casualties while Hamas tries to maximize them, because they realized that their own civilians dying makes Israel look bad. Are these the people you're siding with? I don't know why so many people started repeating this line that Israel is "indiscriminately bombing Palestinians". Maybe they just heard somebody else say it and they're repeating it without even really thinking about what it means, because I've actually stopped many conversations to ask people what they think it means, and then to provide any evidence that it's happening, and in most cases they either didn't know the meaning or had no idea how to substantiate it. They just said it. Joe Biden said this at some point, by the way, as early as December 2023. "[Israel] has most of the world supporting it", but "they're starting to lose that support by the indiscriminate bombing that takes place." He doesn't elaborate and nobody challenges or questions that statement, so it just randomly went on record, and a bunch of people saw a headline of Biden using that word. He may have significantly contributed to its use. The idea that Israel is just randomly throwing bombs into Gaza without regard for who the bombs hit may have come from the notion that Israel uses dumb bombs, and misunderstandings about the implications of that very fact. A dumb bomb is an unguided munition, which is what all munitions were until we invented guided munitions, or smart bombs, in the mid-1900s, which had an onboard computer and sensor system that can steer itself. It didn't become common to actually utilize them until the 90s, and now they're becoming more normal---but we obviously don't think that all bombing prior to the last few decades was 'indiscriminate'. That would be an insane thing to claim. I'm not a munitions expert, but I get the sense the difference between using guided and unguided munitions isn't as great as people think when you're just blowing up a large, stationary target like a building. And for some reasons, Israel has been denied shipments of smart munitions by some countries, including the US, leaving them with only access to unguided munitions. (By the way, indiscriminate bombing is exactly what Gazans do to Israelis any time they can.) ### The pager attack In 2024, a bunch of pagers exploded in Lebanon. The next day, walkie-talkies. They were specifically the devices sold to members of Hezbollah, the terrorist group that's taken over southern Lebanon. I recall people messaging me after that attack, asking what I thought, hoping that I'd condemn it. Not only do I not condemn it, but I don't see why it's not preferable to any other method of attack Israel could've pursued. They figured out a way to precision-target Hezbollah members: target the devices they were using to communicate with each other. It's a creative way of distinguishing between the actual militants and the civilian population. I realize there were some civilian injuries, but overall very few, and it's not like there's ever zero in war. Imagine we had the opportunity to bomb a house where all the top Nazis were having a meeting, but there were also some civilian cooks or other servants attending who would die. That would *obviously* be seen as an unfortunately cost necessary to take out valid military targets. I remember some of the most anti-Israel people calling it a terrorist attack. It was *literally* terrifying in the sense it scared people, perhaps. If I were walking down the street, and I didn't realize the guy in front of me was a literal terrorist, and his phone suddenly exploded, then yeah, I might be freaked out a bit. But that's not actually what terrorism is. I read an article by somebody who said after this attack, every single person in the Middle East has to be scared their devices might blow up at any time if the IDF wants them dead. This is only true in the stupidest sense possible. If there's a war, and the enemy planes bomb a legitimate military target, like a military outpost full of soldiers in our military, then should I be scared that my house might be next? It could get bombed in the same way, by those same planes. It could happen. They have planes. They could fly over here and blow me up in my house. They could bomb my neighbourhood. By this logic, virtually any act of war is an act of terrorism, because it's a demonstration that you *could* attack the civilian population instead, and that makes them scared. This reaction was just a way to spin the situation against Israel, even though it's an instance of Israel doing the exact opposite of targeting the civilian population, the exact opposite of the thing everybody is always trying to accuse them of (not caring about the militant--civilian distinction). ## How to move forward ### What could Israel do to end the conflict? Israel's current borders weren't agreed to diplomatically. They were armistice lines, meaning the implicit boundaries between sides during a war, like what you might see on a map of Europe half-way through WWII, or a map of the Russia--Ukraine war right now. When the fighting stopped, the dust settled, and that's where the lines remained. Israel didn't have the population nor military might to expand and hold territory without limit. They weren't conquerors. They could just barely manage to keep the land they inhabited. **The situation Israel ended up in was this:** there are two regions on either side of the country they can't control, conquer, deport, or police. And the population of these two places both want to declare war on you and kill all your people. What do you do? It's a situation they can't deal with. A situation of their own making, you might say, but after some point that doesn't matter---they don't have a time machine to change the past. What do they do now? For the latter part of the 20th century, there was basically nothing Israel could have done to create peace short of self-destruction, either in the form of all leaving the region or giving up on their political independence and allowing the region to be overtaken by a Muslim majority---which, given their experience in Europe after the Holocaust, and given the violently anti-Jewish nature of the population in question here, was obviously and understandably unacceptable. Ideally separation should eventually have led to peace, but that's not how things work out if one side is completely unsatisfied with separation because they demand your defeat at all costs and believe they have the power to accomplish that. Today a solution proposed by critics of Israel is for Israel to end their blockade of Gaza and withdraw all presence from the West Bank, at least. These are agreeable goals, but Israel doesn't do these things for no reason. A huge portion of the people in Gaza do want to kill them all. The region does descend into being ruled by organized terrorist groups who want to wage against Israel. They take in substantial resources from outside, and they funnel those resources into trying to build bombs and other weapons, which they use to try and murder random Israelis. The Israeli blockade isn't there simply as a means to harm Gaza and keep them down; it's self-defensively motivated. Their presence in the West Bank is similar, except they can't possibly blockade that region as they can with Gaza. And for the people of Israel---who feel they're at war and that these people want to exterminate them and have been terrorizing their side for decades---simply backing off like this is a big ask. When a nation is attacked and given a *casus belli*, we don't demand they sit on their hands, even though that *would* result in peace. It becomes unfortunately unrealistic: almost nobody who feels they've just been attacked simply values peace. They want to respond. They care about the precedent being set for the future, they demand answers, they want to know this isn't just going to happen again. If you forgive and capitulate and appease, the problem often gets worse, not better. And that would be the case here. If Israel ended their blockade of Gaza, would the result be peace, or Hamas importing more weapons and getting ready for their next attack? This does get more complicated with the West Bank, as there are some elements within Israel who want to rule the West Bank for religious, historical regions, even referring to this as "Greater Israel", which it's implied they have some kind of Biblical claim to. There are, moreover, people within Israel who feel total withdraw from the West Bank would be insufficient and solve nothing---those who want them dead before would still want them dead afterwards. It would be surrendering strategic territory for essentially no reason. The religious aspect of Israeli politics that aspires to reclaim some kind of historical Judea *is* a problem, but it's not also the dominant, majority voice in the country. It's just one of many voices present. The US Congress has some unreasonable people in it, too. Gaza and the West Bank, when left to their own devices, fall apart into chaos and get taken over by extremist groups. They aren't stable regions, they don't have functioning institutions, the population is young and susceptible to radicalization. How do you fix this? There probably needs to be some kind of external, stabilizing element, and that being Israel itself is going to be rejected. It would be nice if Egypt, Jordan, or any other more stable Arab country that could control this population and prevent them from being terrorists and funnelling all their resources into trying to shoot rockets at Israelis, but those countries have no interest in doing this, because---as noted before---they would prefer the Palestinians linger around like this because it puts endless pressure on Israel, and because they don't want the trouble of dealing with the Palestinians in their own countries, nor committing the resources to come in and govern these places. By the 21st century, Arab countries' attitudes toward Israel have shifted. Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Egypt, Lebanon---the actual governments of these other countries have largely abandoned the hope that if they carry on fighting, forever they'll eventually destroy Israel. The people who started these wars have died of old age; the people now in charge were born into conflict. They've become more interested in moving on, finding a way, and normalizing relations---and diversifying their economies. Things haven't fully developed in that way yet (*officially*, some of these countries, like Saudi Arabia, still don't even recognize Israel as a country), but that's the direction it's moved in, and is still moving in. For this reason, I feel there's more opportunity now, in theory, for Israel to normalize relations with the Palestinians by trying to directly help develop and improve these regions---but they likely can't force this outcome by theirselves. What should Israel do? Leave the West Bank, you say. But if they leave the West Bank, what happens? If Israel up and leaves, the people in the West Bank start prepping for war. It just kicks the can down the road, and by the time you catch up with it again, the problem will be worse, not better. This is the bind Israel finds itself in: the conditions for their military occupation to end aren't being met. They can't just leave, they can't absorb the territory, and they can't expel everybody out of the territory, at least not without massive international condemnation. Here, like on other fronts, it can feel there's no right answer. ### What could Palestinians do to end the conflict? This part of the conversation is difficult because the Palestinian population is generally treated as though it has no agency. And there is some truth to that. There are some valid reasons for why they do have less agency than other populations. For one, the average age in the region is very low. They're less educated, less organized, less wealthy, and frankly they've been propagandized for the last few generations. They have television, but they also have TV stations run by terrorist networks. They have children's programming created by fascistic Jihadists whose goal is teaching children born into Gaza about the evil of Jews. This population is not in a good state economically, socially, or mentally. So what can I realistically ask of them? This is an unfortunate barrier because a change in Palestinian people would be more impactful than similar changes among any other population. They have the most potential. If Arab leadership outside of Palestine (and to clarify: 'Palestine' means Gaza and the West Bank) wanted to make this fabled "peace in the Middle East" happen, a key to reaching that outcome is figuring out how to regulate, deradicalize, and control the Palestinians---not to enslave them, but to control them as the governments of other countries control their own populations, by enforcing laws, and preventing violent paramilitary groups from running the streets and indoctrinating children, to prevent people from pouring all their resources into making rockets because they've come to believe their main purpose in life is trying to murder Jews. The conversation ends up feeling like we're talking about a friend who's a drug addict. We can say "they need to quit the drugs" all day long. And in a simple sense, that's true. We talk about how many people there are in Palestine below the age of 18, but there's also plenty of adults. It's not impossible for people there to move in the direction of saying they don't want this to go on forever, and they need to recognize a different path to go down, one that isn't infinite, endless Jihad and warfare. If Hamas and groups like them weren't regularly proving the need for Israel's policies, eventually the public would say, hey, wait, why are we blockading these people? What's the point? And it would be taken down. ### The two-state solution The state-state solution sounds like the most obvious thing ever. It's what you're supposed to say if you just want to be normal, uncontroversial, and signal that you value life on both sides. It almost feels like you're not even taking a side. There's two populations that don't get along, but they reside in two separate territories with a clear boundary between them. Why not just declare these separate countries, let them organize their own governments, and have peace through separation? In the past, Israel was willing to do that. It was the Arabs who opposed it. The Arabs always wanted *more*---more than whatever was offered to them.
